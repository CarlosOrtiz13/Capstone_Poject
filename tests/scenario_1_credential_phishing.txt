On Tuesday morning, the finance department of a mid-sized healthcare provider in Virginia began reporting suspicious Microsoft 365 login prompts that appeared immediately after employees clicked a link in what looked like an internal password reset email. The sender display name was "IT Support Team," but the underlying email address used an external domain that resembled the company’s legitimate help desk address.

Within two hours, several employees entered their usernames, passwords, and multi-factor authentication codes into the fake login page. Security analysts later determined that the phishing page was hosted on a recently registered domain and proxied traffic to the legitimate Microsoft 365 login portal in real time, allowing the attacker to capture session tokens.

Using the compromised accounts, the threat actor accessed multiple mailboxes belonging to payroll and accounts payable personnel. The attacker created hidden inbox rules that forwarded messages containing keywords such as "invoice," "payment," and "wire" to an external account. They also sent follow-up phishing emails from an internal compromised mailbox to other employees, increasing the credibility of the campaign.

Logs showed successful logins from IP addresses associated with a VPS provider in another country, followed by abnormal mailbox access and message searches. Analysts also observed that one account attempted to access the company’s SharePoint environment and downloaded spreadsheets containing vendor banking information. No ransomware was deployed, but the organization believes the objective was business email compromise and financial fraud.

The company disabled the affected accounts, reset passwords, revoked active sessions, and blocked the phishing domain. Investigators are still unsure whether the same actor has targeted other healthcare organizations, but they suspect a financially motivated group specializing in credential phishing and mailbox abuse.
